# Benchmark Documentation

## Source Dataset

The benchmark audio clips are derived from the **LibriSpeech ASR corpus**, specifically the `test-clean` subset.

- **Dataset:** LibriSpeech ASR corpus (Panayotov et al., 2015)
- **Subset:** `test-clean` (346 MB)
- **Download URL:** https://www.openslr.org/resources/12/test-clean.tar.gz
- **License:** CC BY 4.0
- **Audio format (original):** FLAC, 16 kHz, mono, 16-bit PCM

LibriSpeech was created from audiobooks read by volunteers through the LibriVox project. The `test-clean` subset contains read English speech from speakers with clear pronunciation and low background noise. Transcripts were derived from the original book text and manually verified.

## Speaker and Chapter

All three benchmark clips come from a single speaker and chapter to ensure consistent acoustic conditions (same microphone, same room, same voice):

- **Speaker ID:** 1089
- **Chapter ID:** 134686
- **Source text:** *A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man* by James Joyce (public domain)

The original utterances are stored at:
```
audio/librispeech/LibriSpeech/test-clean/1089/134686/
```

Each utterance is a `.flac` file. Ground-truth transcripts are stored in the accompanying `1089-134686.trans.txt` file, with one line per utterance in the format:

```
1089-134686-0000 HE HOPED THERE WOULD BE STEW FOR DINNER TURNIPS AND CARROTS...
```

## Clip Construction

The three benchmark clips were generated by the script at `scripts/prepare_audio.py`. The construction process was:

1. **Select utterances** from the chapter transcript file to reach the target duration for each clip.
2. **Load each utterance** from `.flac` using `librosa.load()`, resampled to 16 kHz mono float32.
3. **Concatenate** the raw audio arrays using `numpy.concatenate()` — no silence padding or crossfade was added between utterances.
4. **Write** the result to a 16 kHz mono WAV file using `soundfile.write()`.
5. **Concatenate transcripts** by joining the individual utterance strings with a single space.

## Noise Conditions

Two types of noise are applied to the clean audio before transcription. Each type simulates a different real-world degradation scenario and stresses the model in a fundamentally different way.

### Gaussian Noise

Gaussian (white) noise is randomly generated samples drawn from a normal distribution, added directly to the audio waveform. It is spectrally flat — energy is distributed uniformly across all frequencies — and has no memory or temporal structure. Each sample is independent of the samples around it.

The noise level is controlled by **SNR (Signal-to-Noise Ratio)** in decibels, defined as:

```
SNR (dB) = 20 · log10(RMS_signal / RMS_noise)
```

A higher SNR means the signal is louder relative to the noise. 0 dB means the noise RMS equals the signal RMS — noise is as loud as speech. Negative SNR would mean noise is louder than speech.

| SNR | Perceptual description |
|---|---|
| 40 dB | Essentially clean — noise barely perceptible |
| 20 dB | Slight background hiss, speech very clear |
| 10 dB | Noticeable noise, speech still intelligible |
| 5 dB | Heavy noise, requires effort to follow |
| 0 dB | Noise as loud as speech, very difficult |

Whisper is robust to Gaussian noise because: (1) it was trained on 680,000 hours of diverse internet audio that included noisy recordings; (2) log-mel spectrograms compress the impact of additive noise; and (3) flat-spectrum noise does not destroy the temporal structure of speech — phonemes remain distinguishable even under heavy noise.

**Implementation:** `benchmark/noise.py → add_gaussian()`. Noise is scaled to the target SNR using the RMS of the clean signal as reference, then added to the waveform.

### Room Impulse Response (RIR)

In a real room, sound travels from a speaker to a microphone via multiple paths: a direct path, and many reflected paths bouncing off walls, floors, ceilings, and furniture. Each reflection arrives slightly later and quieter than the direct sound. The combined effect is reverberation — a smearing of the signal in time where each sound persists as a decaying echo.

A **Room Impulse Response** is the complete acoustic fingerprint of a room: the signal a microphone would record if an instantaneous impulse (a perfect click) were played in that room. Convolving clean speech with a RIR produces audio that sounds as if the speech were recorded in that room.

RIR is a fundamentally more damaging distortion than Gaussian noise because it destroys temporal structure. Each phoneme's energy bleeds into the frames that follow it, causing previous speech to interfere with current speech. Unlike additive noise, the model cannot simply learn to ignore it — the signal itself is scrambled.

The severity of reverb is measured by **RT60** — the time in seconds for sound energy to decay by 60 dB after the source stops. Higher RT60 means longer-lasting echoes.

| RT60 | Typical environment |
|---|---|
| 0.2s | Small treated room, recording booth |
| 0.5s | Average living room or office |
| 1.0s | Large unfurnished room, classroom |
| 2.0s | Church, concert hall, large warehouse |

**Room dimensions** in this benchmark are fixed at 6.0 m × 5.0 m × 3.0 m (length × width × height), representing a medium-sized room. The source (speaker) is placed at [1.8, 3.0, 1.5] m and the microphone at [4.2, 2.0, 1.5] m. Wall absorption coefficients are derived from the target RT60 using Sabine's formula, and the impulse response is synthesized using the image source method.

**Implementation:** `benchmark/noise.py → add_rir()`, using the `pyroomacoustics` library. The synthesized RIR is convolved with the signal using `scipy.signal.fftconvolve`.

## Models

Eight models were selected to benchmark three independent axes: **model size** (small vs. large), **inference backend** (PyTorch/MPS, MLX, CTranslate2 CPU, HuggingFace pipeline), and **training approach** (standard Whisper vs. knowledge distillation). All models transcribe the same audio under the same noise and preprocessing conditions.

### openai/small and openai/large-v3
**Source:** OpenAI Whisper (Radford et al., 2022)
**HuggingFace / weights:** Downloaded automatically by the `openai-whisper` Python package.
**Backend:** PyTorch, using Apple's MPS (Metal Performance Shaders) GPU backend on M1.

The original Whisper models released by OpenAI. `small` has 244M parameters; `large-v3` has 1.55B parameters and is the highest-accuracy model in the Whisper family. These serve as the baseline — standard PyTorch inference on the M1 GPU via MPS. MPS is a general-purpose GPU compute layer and is not specifically optimized for transformer inference on Apple Silicon.

---

### mlx/small and mlx/large-v3
**Source:** MLX community ports of OpenAI Whisper weights
**HuggingFace repos:** `mlx-community/whisper-small-mlx`, `mlx-community/whisper-large-v3-mlx`
**Backend:** Apple MLX framework, targeting the Apple Neural Engine (ANE) and GPU natively.

These use the same underlying weights as the openai models but run through Apple's MLX framework, which is designed specifically for Apple Silicon. MLX dispatches computation across the CPU, GPU, and Neural Engine through the unified memory architecture, avoiding the copies between memory pools that PyTorch/MPS requires. The direct comparison between `openai/large-v3` and `mlx/large-v3` isolates the effect of the inference backend with all other variables held constant.

---

### faster/small-int8 and faster/large-v3-int8
**Source:** Systran faster-whisper
**Backend:** CTranslate2, running entirely on CPU with INT8 quantization.

Faster-whisper converts Whisper weights to CTranslate2 format and quantizes them from float32 to INT8, reducing model size and memory bandwidth requirements at the cost of a small precision loss. Unlike the openai and mlx variants, these run with no GPU involvement — all inference happens on the M1 CPU cores. INT8 quantization allows CTranslate2 to use SIMD integer instructions that are significantly faster than float32 operations on CPU. This provides a data point for CPU-only deployment scenarios and shows the accuracy cost of quantization.

---

### distil/small and distil/large-v3
**Source:** Distil-Whisper (Gandhi et al., 2023), HuggingFace
**HuggingFace repos:** `distil-whisper/distil-small.en`, `distil-whisper/distil-large-v3`
**Backend:** PyTorch + MPS via HuggingFace `transformers` pipeline.

Distil-Whisper is a family of models produced by knowledge distillation from Whisper large-v3. The student models have fewer decoder layers and were trained to match the output distribution of the teacher. `distil-large-v3` is approximately 6× faster than `large-v3` with a small accuracy penalty; `distil-small.en` is an English-only model distilled to the small parameter budget. Unlike the faster-whisper models, distillation changes the model architecture itself rather than just the numerical precision of the weights. Note that `distil-small.en` is English-only and will not generalize to other languages.

### Why `faster/small-int8` and `distil/small` Are Slower Than Expected

Both models show worse RTF than `openai/small` despite having similar or smaller parameter counts. The reasons differ:

**`faster/small-int8`** runs entirely on the CPU — no GPU is used. INT8 quantization reduces the weight size and enables faster integer SIMD operations on CPU, but the M1 GPU (accessed via MPS or MLX) is substantially faster than the M1 CPU for the matrix multiplications that dominate transformer inference. The quantization advantage does not overcome the GPU vs. CPU gap on this hardware. `faster/small-int8` is the correct choice for CPU-only deployment scenarios, but on M1 it cannot match a GPU-accelerated model.

**`distil/small`** runs through the HuggingFace `transformers` ASR pipeline, which is a much heavier abstraction than the direct inference path used by `openai/small`. Each call involves Python-level chunking logic, a tokenizer, a feature extractor, multiple dispatch layers, and the `chunk_length_s=30` chunking mechanism required for audio longer than 30 seconds. The underlying distilled model has fewer parameters (~166M vs 244M for small), but the framework overhead is larger. This demonstrates that inference speed and model size are not the same — the bottleneck here is the runtime, not the weights.

`mlx/small` achieves the best RTF (0.071×) because it has the least overhead: direct MLX calls with no pipeline abstraction, native dispatch to the Apple Neural Engine and GPU, and an allocator designed for the M1 unified memory architecture.

### Noise × Preprocessing — Effect on WER (openai/small)

All combinations of noise condition × preprocessing pipeline × clip were tested. The table below shows average WER across all three clips.

| Noise | Baseline | VAD | Spectral | RMS | Best delta |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Gaussian SNR=0 dB | 0.113 | 0.199 | 0.369 | 0.106 | −0.007 |
| Gaussian SNR=5 dB | 0.033 | 0.038 | 0.094 | 0.031 | −0.002 |
| Gaussian SNR=10 dB | 0.017 | 0.012 | 0.076 | 0.017 | −0.005 |
| Gaussian SNR=20 dB | 0.014 | 0.012 | 0.021 | 0.017 | −0.002 |
| Gaussian SNR=40 dB | 0.017 | 0.007 | 0.064 | 0.017 | −0.010 |
| RIR RT60=0.2s | 0.014 | 0.012 | 0.118 | 0.014 | −0.002 |
| RIR RT60=0.5s | 0.012 | 0.012 | 0.542 | 0.012 | 0.000 |
| RIR RT60=1.0s | 0.227 | 0.364 | 0.968 | 0.227 | 0.000 |
| RIR RT60=2.0s | 0.753 | 0.842 | 0.967 | **0.632** | **−0.121** |

**Key findings:**

- Whisper is largely robust to Gaussian noise at all SNR levels tested. Preprocessing provides at most marginal gains (< 1 percentage point) in the Gaussian noise conditions. This is consistent with Whisper's training on 680,000 hours of diverse internet audio, which included noisy recordings.
- Spectral gating consistently degrades accuracy. On clean or lightly noisy audio it corrupts speech that had no noise to begin with. At RT60=0.5s it raises WER from 0.012 to 0.542. It should only ever be applied when there is strong stationary background noise.
- VAD provides small improvements at moderate SNR but worsens results at high reverb (RT60=1.0–2.0s). Heavy reverberation causes the VAD model to misclassify reverb tails as speech and silence as non-speech, degrading the segmentation.
- **RMS normalization is the only pipeline that shows a meaningful improvement: −12.1 percentage points at RT60=2.0s (0.753 → 0.632).** At extreme reverb, amplitude variation across the audio becomes severe and RMS normalisation stabilises the input level before Whisper's mel spectrogram computation.

### RMS Normalisation vs Room Size at RT60=2.0s

The RMS improvement at RT60=2.0s was tested across four room sizes to determine whether the acoustic environment affects the result.

| Room | Dimensions | Volume | Baseline WER | RMS WER | Improvement |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| small_office | 3×3×2.5m | 22 m³ | 0.566 | 0.529 | −0.037 |
| medium_room | 6×5×3m | 90 m³ | 0.753 | 0.632 | −0.121 |
| large_office | 10×8×4m | 320 m³ | 0.631 | 0.444 | **−0.187** |
| lecture_hall | 15×12×5m | 900 m³ | 0.350 | 0.339 | −0.012 |

RMS normalisation improved over baseline in every room. The largest gain was in the large_office (−18.7 percentage points). The lecture hall saw the smallest gain because its baseline WER is already the lowest.

**Why baseline WER varies by room size at the same RT60:**

Two acoustic mechanisms are at work:

1. **Direct-to-reverberant ratio (DRR).** The source and microphone are placed at proportionally equivalent positions in each room. In the small office the source-mic distance is ~1.3m; in the lecture hall it is ~6.3m. Direct sound attenuates as 1/distance², while the reverberant field is roughly uniform throughout the room. A shorter source-mic distance means the direct speech signal is stronger relative to the reverb, improving intelligibility.

2. **Reflection density.** For the same RT60, a larger room has a longer mean free path — the average distance sound travels between wall reflections. This means reflections arrive less frequently per unit time, creating a more spread-out reverb with less temporal smearing of phonemes. A smaller room packs far more reflections into the same time window, which is more damaging to ASR.

The medium room sits in an awkward position: the source-mic distance is moderate (losing the DRR advantage of the small room) while the reflection density is high enough to cause severe speech smearing. This explains why it shows the worst baseline WER despite being neither the smallest nor the largest space.
